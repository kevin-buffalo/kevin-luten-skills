# Voice Components: What to Capture, What to Ignore

This file defines exactly what belongs in a voice profile and what does not. Read it for the Describe movement (Step 5). The single most common failure mode is letting structural/copywriting advice leak into a voice profile, so the boundary is drawn sharply here.

## The reading method: find what's conspicuous against ordinary writing

Voice is not "everything the person does" — it's what they do *differently from ordinary writing*. A trait only counts as voice if it departs from how a generically competent writer would handle the same thing. So the close-read is a search for **departures from typical**, not a catalogue of everything present.

On the **full path**, you don't have to rely on instinct for this: the Discover movement (Step 4, `measurement.md`) has already measured the user's samples against published English norms and handed you an objective list of deviations — function words used far above or below normal, sentence lengths, punctuation rates. Use that list as the spine of the read. Your job in this movement is to take those measured departures and explain *how they combine into a recognizable voice*, anchored in real excerpts. Measurement found *that* sentences fracture and semicolons are absent; you explain that this produces a terse, declarative voice that lands hard and refuses warmup — the thing the user will recognize.

On the **reduced path** (no measurement), you perform the same search by reading: scan each component dimension below and ask, for each, "is this different from how most writing handles it?" The implicit baseline is ordinary modern prose — sentences averaging ~20 words, semicolons present, openings that warm up, balanced punctuation. Note only the conspicuous departures, and be honest that without measurement this read is your best judgment rather than a verified count.

Either way, the output is the same kind of portrait: distinctive traits, concretely described, each tied to evidence in the samples.

## The principle

**Voice** is *how* a person writes, independent of *what* they are writing or *why*. It's the fingerprint that stays constant whether they're writing a sales page or a condolence note. Most of it is unconscious — people don't decide their average sentence length or their comma habits; those just are what they are.

**Structure** is *what* a piece of writing does to accomplish a goal — its rhetorical architecture. The same writer uses wildly different structures for different tasks while their voice stays the same. Structure is a deliberate, task-dependent choice.

A voice profile captures the first and says nothing about the second. The test: *would this trait stay the same if the person wrote a completely different kind of document?* If yes, it's voice. If it changes with the task, it's structure — leave it out.

## Capture these (voice)

Describe each one concretely, with evidence from the samples. Vague descriptors are useless; specifics are everything.


- **Sentence length and variation.** Average length, and — more telling — the *rhythm*: do they write uniformly medium sentences, or alternate long winding ones with short punches? Do they use fragments? When?
- **Punctuation habits.** Em dashes (how often, ever doubled?), semicolons (used or avoided entirely?), colons, parentheticals, ellipses, comma density. These are strong, stable fingerprints.
- **Vocabulary level and register.** Plain vs. elevated, Anglo-Saxon vs. Latinate, concrete vs. abstract. The reading-grade feel.
- **Specific word preferences and allergies.** Words and phrases they reach for repeatedly; words and phrases they would never use. Capture *their* actual ones from the samples — e.g., "always writes 'folks' never 'guys'; says 'roughly' not 'approximately'; never uses 'super' as an intensifier." These are personal fingerprints, not a generic list of words-to-avoid; pull them from evidence in the writing.
- **Function-word and contraction patterns.** Contractions always / never / sometimes. Pet connectives ("and so," "but here's the thing," "look,"). How they start sentences (with "And"/"But"? never?).
- **Formality and how it shifts.** Where they sit on the formal–casual axis, and — if relevant to the named context — how that dial moves with audience (this is the one genuinely register-sensitive trait worth noting).
- **Hedging vs. directness.** Do they qualify heavily ("it seems," "perhaps," "I think") or state flatly? Do they soften or land hard?
- **Person and stance.** First / second / third person habits; how present the writer is; whether they address the reader directly.
- **Figurative language.** Whether they use metaphor/analogy at all, and if so, what *kind* — homely and concrete, or abstract and grand. Their characteristic comparisons.
- **Paragraph density and white space.** Long blocks vs. airy single-sentence paragraphs. (This is a voice habit when it's consistent across everything they write — distinguish it from deliberate post-formatting, which is structure.)
- **Tics and signatures.** Any repeated, idiosyncratic move that marks their writing — a characteristic aside, a way of conceding a point, a recurring rhetorical question used as *texture* rather than as a structural device.

## Ignore these (structure / copywriting)

None of these belong in a voice profile. They are task-dependent choices handled by copywriting/content tools, not by a voice reference.

- Opening with a hook, and which type of hook
- Persuasion frameworks (AIDA, PAS, problem-agitate-solution, StoryBrand, etc.)
- Where the call-to-action goes, or whether there is one
- Listicle vs. narrative vs. essay structure
- Section ordering, headers as an organizing device
- Using a contrarian/provocative claim to grab attention
- Length targets for a given format
- SEO, keyword placement, platform-specific formatting conventions

If the user *wants* help with these, that's a separate, legitimate task — point them to it, but keep it out of the voice profile, because baking structure into the profile makes it non-portable: it would force a hook onto a reflective essay or a framework onto a quick reply.

## A note on the gray zone

Some traits sit near the line. Resolve them with the test above:

- *"He uses lots of rhetorical questions."* — Voice **if** it's a constant texture in everything he writes (a verbal habit). Structure **if** it's a deliberate engagement device he deploys in posts but not emails. Look at the evidence across contexts before deciding.
- *"Short paragraphs with white space."* — Voice if consistent everywhere; structure if it's clearly LinkedIn-post formatting he wouldn't use in an email. When unsure, note it as a voice tendency but don't state it as an absolute rule.
- *"Starts with a personal anecdote."* — Almost always structure (a content choice), not voice. Leave it out.

When genuinely unsure, prefer to describe the *texture* (voice) rather than the *move* (structure). "Tends to write in short, punchy units" is voice; "opens with a one-line hook" is structure.

### The most common leak — watch for it

The trap that catches even careful analysts is **organizational habits disguised as voice.** These describe how a person *arranges* a piece, which is structure, and they slip in because they feel personal. The following are STRUCTURE — exclude them, no matter how characteristic they seem:

- "Leads with the point / the conclusion first" (BLUF) — organizational choice
- "Puts a TL;DR or summary at the top" — formatting/organizational choice
- "Explains *why* before *how*" — sequencing choice
- "Builds problem → solution → result" — narrative architecture
- "Ends with a takeaway or call to action" — structural close

If you find yourself writing any of these into the profile, stop: you've crossed from *how they sound* into *how they organize*. The same person organizes a tweet and a report completely differently while sounding identical in both. Capture the sound, not the arrangement.
